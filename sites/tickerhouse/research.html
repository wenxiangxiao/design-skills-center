CRBX — Inference margins are the story, not the training capex

M. Okafor, senior analyst · 2026-07-10 06:40 · 9 min read · note #187 · fictional sample

SUMMARY

The market keeps scoring Cerebrix like a capex arms race: whoever books the most accelerator supply wins the quarter. We think that framing is eighteen months stale. Training capacity is becoming a commodity input; the durable spread sits in inference serving, where Cerebrix's compiler stack turns identical hardware into 31% operating margin versus a peer set stuck in the high teens.

We initiate at OVERWEIGHT with a 12-month target of $505, based on 28x our FY27 inference-segment EBIT plus net cash. That multiple is not cheap. It is, in our view, cheaper than the alternative of modeling this company off training revenue that everyone can see decelerating.

THESIS

  • Inference revenue doubles twice by FY27 on our numbers, and mix alone adds ~490bp of gross margin without any pricing heroics.
  • The compiler moat is measurable: in our teardown, identical models served through the Cerebrix stack used 34% fewer accelerator-hours than the best open toolchain. Customers churn at 4% annually. That is infrastructure behavior, not software-tools behavior.
  • Consensus still books 55% of FY27 revenue as training. We book 42%. If we are right, consensus is modeling the wrong company.

VALUATION

Sum-of-parts: inference EBIT of $956M in FY27 at 28x = $437/share; training and other at a deliberately dull 9x = $46/share; net cash $22/share. Total $505. Bears will call 28x rich — we would answer that the last three infrastructure businesses with sub-5% churn and 30%+ margins never traded below 25x forward EBIT for longer than a quarter.

RISKS

  • A hyperscaler open-sources a comparable serving stack and prices it at zero. We assign this a real probability; it caps our multiple, not our thesis.
